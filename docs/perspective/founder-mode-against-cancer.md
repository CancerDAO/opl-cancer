# Democratizing the Patient's Research Team, with Discipline: AI, Founder Mode, and the Pain-A/Pain-B Decomposition in Exhausted-Options Cancer

**CancerDAO Contributors**

---

## Abstract

When standard-of-care is exhausted, a cancer patient confronts an options desert, an information asymmetry, and an agency vacuum. A few patients — Sid Sijbrandij, David Fajgenbaum — escaped by going "founder mode": becoming the principal investigator of their own case. Each reported outcome required elite resources (a chief executive's budget, a physician's laboratory), so the archetype is at best a proven possibility but an unscalable one. We argue that AI can in principle democratize founder-mode's *method* via a patient's "AI scientist team," but only under a discipline: decompose the need into Pain-A (retrieve, match, and vet the real options that already exist — the molecular tumor board's job) executed first and exhaustively, then Pain-B (grounded novel-lead generation) fenced as exploratory. The literature strongly supports this discipline — large language models fabricate worst in the rare and recurrent regime where these patients live, while retrieval grounding with verifiable attribution restores guideline concordance toward ceiling — but it is thin on the democratization payoff: no published study yet shows AI closing the resource gap for an ordinary patient, and the precision-oncology benefit on which the enterprise leans is associative, with one negative randomized trial. A small, synthetic benchmark of one concrete instantiation (OPL) shows that disciplined retrieval-first synthesis can improve option selection, while independently surfacing a "know-when-to-stop" overreach failure. The evidence today licenses the method, not yet the payoff.

---

## 1. Two Operating Modes

When Sid Sijbrandij, the chief executive of GitLab, was told that his osteosarcoma had recurred and that no clinical trial fit his case, he faced a choice between two operating modes, in the language of the start-up world he came from. In *manager mode*, a patient trusts the institutions around them — their oncologist, their cancer centre, the referral network — to surface whatever options exist and to route them to the right one. This is the default, and for patients within the reach of standard-of-care it is usually adequate. For Sijbrandij it had already failed: the institutional machinery had run to its end and returned an empty set. What he did instead was switch into *founder mode*. Rather than wait for a system to deliver him an option, he set out to build an operation to manufacture one. By his own account he assembled a multidisciplinary team of specialists, commissioned single-cell and bulk RNA sequencing of his tumour alongside personalized immune profiling, travelled internationally to access expertise and procedures unavailable at any single site, and maintained what amounted to an N-of-1 database on his own disease. In effect, he made himself the principal investigator of his own case, and he has reported reaching no evidence of disease.

It would be easy to read this as a fortunate anecdote — a desperate gamble that happened to pay off — but the more useful reading is that it instantiates a recognizable and, crucially, a *disciplined* archetype. The cleanest documented precedent is David Fajgenbaum, a physician-scientist who, dying of idiopathic multicentric Castleman disease with no approved therapy, profiled his own samples, identified mTOR hyperactivation, and repurposed an already-approved drug, sirolimus, to achieve a remission that proved durable in his own case and that he then escalated into a formal clinical trial (Fajgenbaum et al. 2019). What distinguishes this from quackery is not the audacity of the move but its grounding: a novel lead anchored in the patient's own molecular data and in an asset that already existed in the world, vetted and then subjected to formal evidence-generation rather than asserted. Founder mode, done well, is not the abandonment of rigour; it is the relocation of the research enterprise to the individual case.

Both Sijbrandij and Fajgenbaum reported favourable outcomes — Fajgenbaum's documented in the peer-reviewed literature, Sijbrandij's by self-report in journalistic accounts — and both reports share a property that should trouble anyone who wants to generalize from them: each required capability that almost no patient possesses. Sijbrandij commanded the resources of a chief executive; Fajgenbaum brought the training of a physician and access to a laboratory. The relevant question, therefore, is not whether founder mode can work — these selected existence proofs suggest it can — but whether the *method* can be separated from the *means*. Can the disciplined assembly and direction of a research team be democratized to a patient who has neither a CEO's budget nor an MD's lab, without collapsing into false hope? That question turns on two separate conditions, and it is important not to conflate them: imposing the right discipline, which the literature supports, and actually closing the resource gap, which remains unproven. This article argues that the first condition is well grounded and that the discipline is a specific one — doing the molecular tumour board's job, finding and vetting the real options that already exist, before and as a precondition for generating any novel ones — while treating the second as the central open question.

## 2. The Problem: An Options Desert, an Information Asymmetry, and an Agency Vacuum

When a patient exhausts standard-of-care, the difficulty is rarely that no further options exist anywhere in the world; it is that the options that do exist are scattered, gated, and unreachable. We characterize this predicament along three intersecting axes — an *options desert*, an *information asymmetry*, and an *agency vacuum* — and argue that the manual solution that resolved them for Sijbrandij cannot, by construction, be generalized.

**The options desert is structural, not just biological.** Even inside a fully resourced molecular tumor board (MTB) — the institutional apparatus purpose-built to convert molecular data into treatment — only about one in five patients who enter the process actually receives the recommended therapy (Frost et al. 2022). The attrition is instructive: roughly 22% have no actionable mutation, 15% deteriorate clinically before a recommendation can be acted on, and 31% of studies report the specific failure of an *actionable mutation with no available drug* (Frost et al. 2022). The deterioration figure is the clearest agency-and-time failure rather than a discovery of futility — patients effectively die in the queue — while the no-actionable-mutation figure is a biological finding and the no-available-drug residual marks the precise boundary where the real-option space has been exhausted. The headline option beyond standard-of-care, the clinical trial, reaches almost no one: only an estimated 2–8% of adult cancer patients ever enroll (Unger et al. 2019, ASCO Educational Book), with the dominant barrier reported to be not ineligibility but the absence of a suitable trial at the patient's own site (Unger et al. 2019). The matching problem compounds this: eligibility criteria for lung-cancer trials grew from a median of 17 to 27 per protocol between the late 1980s and the mid 2010s (Garcia et al. 2017), so that adjudicating a single patient against the global registry is a combinatorial task no individual can perform by hand.

**The information asymmetry begins before the first option is even considered.** Comprehensive next-generation sequencing — the substrate on which any precision option depends — is unevenly distributed: most patients are treated in community practices with limited precision-oncology infrastructure, and NGS uptake tracks commercial insurance rather than clinical need (Nature Cancer 2023). Because comprehensive panels surface far more actionable alterations than small ones (81% vs 21%; JCO Precision Oncology 2017), patients who are never broadly sequenced are quietly foreclosed from options that, for them, will never appear to exist. The real options are real, but they are scattered across registries, written predominantly in English, gated behind jurisdiction-specific access pathways, and revised continuously — an asymmetry of information and labour, not of biology.

**The agency vacuum is the human core of the problem.** A patient at this juncture typically wants to decide, but has no team with which to decide and no map of the terrain. The structural default routes them through a single oncologist who is, in practice, the gatekeeper to one institution's menu — and an MTB recommendation, even when produced, is implemented in fewer than a third of cases at the institutional level (PMC7962829). The patient-empowerment lineage long predates AI — the "e-patient" framework was authored by a physician who himself died of multiple myeloma (Ferguson 2007), and patient-led networks have generated genuine evidence, as when a self-organized cohort correctly refuted the hyped lithium-in-ALS result using a matching algorithm and controls (Wicks et al. 2011) — but every such instance was bottlenecked by being human-labour-intensive.

This is exactly the bottleneck Sijbrandij overcame by brute force. But that solution does not scale, and the comparable precedents make the point sharply: Fajgenbaum's disciplined N-of-1 turnaround required his own MD training and laboratory access (Fajgenbaum et al. 2019), and Giusti's patient-founded precision-medicine infrastructure required raising over half a billion dollars (Harvard Business School case 814026). Every documented founder-mode success has been purchased with elite resources. The question, therefore, is not whether founder-mode works against an exhausted-options cancer — it demonstrably can, at least for selected patients — but whether its *method*, as distinct from its prerequisite wealth, can be democratized.

## 3. The Paradigm: Democratizing Founder-Mode via the Patient's AI Scientist Team

If founder-mode is the move that rescued Sijbrandij and Fajgenbaum, its central liability is labour. A molecular tumour board is not a building but a *role-set*: a core team of medical oncologist, molecular pathologist, bioinformatician, and geneticist, convened case-by-case with additional specialists (JCO Precision Oncology 2023), and recent European consensus extends this to a dozen or more contributors per case — adding pharmacologists, radiation oncologists, surgeons, bioethicists, and patient representatives (CAN.HEAL Consortium 2025). That accumulated specialist labour is precisely what makes the founder-mode archetype unscalable, since every documented success required either clinical training and a wet lab or the capital to build one (cf. §2). The wager of this paper is that an AI scientist team — a coordinated set of expert agents reproducing those roles — is hypothesized to compress the labour, not the resources. Whether that compression actually makes the method available to a non-CEO patient is unproven, and we return to it in the discussion; here we specify the discipline without which the proposal collapses into the very quackery it must avoid.

That discipline is a decomposition of the patient's need into two tasks, executed in order. **Pain-A** is the retrieval, matching, and vetting of the *real options that already exist in the world*: next-line regimens, NGS-matched and off-label drugs supported by an evidence compendium, open clinical trials, and lawful access pathways such as expanded access. This is the molecular tumour board's actual job, and it must be done first and exhaustively. **Pain-B** is the generation of grounded *novel* leads — N-of-1 hypotheses, drug repurposing, "world-unknown" candidates — and it is legitimate only on the residual cohort that Pain-A cannot satisfy, clearly fenced as exploratory. This Pain-A → Pain-B fence is the canonical decomposition the rest of the paper invokes.

The ordering is the discipline, not a stylistic preference, for three reasons. First, the two tasks differ sharply in maturity. Pain-A is tractable and verifiable today: trial-matching at near-expert accuracy with sentence-anchored, checkable explanations is already demonstrated (Jin et al. 2024), and the legitimacy of off-label use is itself defined by evidence-graded compendium support rather than by physician improvisation (Conti et al. 2013). Pain-B, by contrast, remains preclinical even in its strongest instances; the multi-agent systems that have produced genuinely novel therapeutic hypotheses validated them only in vitro or in organoids (Gottweis et al. 2026; Robin 2026), and the authors of one such system explicitly state it does not replace a research team or complete the science (Gottweis et al. 2026). To offer a patient a "world-unknown" candidate before exhausting the catalogue of established options is to substitute the speculative for the available — the exact inversion that turns hope into cross-border therapy tourism for unproven interventions, whose documented harms are severe (PMC8890798).

Second, the boundary between the two tasks is empirical, not rhetorical. Even inside a fully resourced board, roughly one in five patients ultimately receives the recommended treatment, and about 31% of studies report an actionable alteration with no available drug (Frost et al. 2022). That residual *is* the Pain-A → Pain-B hand-off: the patient earns the right to speculate only once the real-option space has been demonstrably emptied. Third, the discipline matters most precisely where founder-mode patients live. The N-of-1 literature's default is evidentially sloppy — only 3.48% of studies meet all reporting standards (Batley et al. 2023) — so AI's contribution must be the rigour, not the ambition: pooling and vetting that convert anecdote into signal rather than amplifying it (cf. Gouda et al. 2023). Pain-A-first, with every claim traceable to a verifiable source and the patient retained as sole decision authority, is therefore the line that separates a democratized research team from false hope.

## 4. Method: An AI Scientist Team Executing Pain-A → Pain-B Under a No-Fabrication Discipline

The architectural question is not whether a language model *can* discuss exhausted-options oncology, but whether it can be constrained to do so without manufacturing the very evidence a desperate patient cannot independently check. OPL (the system this article describes) is one concrete instantiation of the Pain-A → Pain-B decomposition: a multi-wave AI scientist team of roughly twenty expert roles, each backed by live data integrators (PubMed, ClinicalTrials.gov, ChiCTR, OncoKB, CIViC), with every patient-facing claim anchored to a retrievable identifier (a PMID, an NCT or ChiCTR registration, a compendium entry) and tagged by a three-tier label — *established*, *exploratory*, or *speculative* — that renders the Pain-A/Pain-B fence visible inside the brief itself rather than leaving it as an authorial convention.

The discipline is non-negotiable because the naive alternative fails worst exactly where founder-mode patients live. Citation fabrication is topic-dependent — roughly 6% for well-covered conditions but 28–29% for rarer ones (JMIR 2025) — and raw GPT-4 NCCN concordance falls to 73.8% on *recurrent* cancers (Tsai et al. 2024), Sijbrandij's precise regime. A patient entering OCR-corrupted records is, moreover, an adversarial prompt: every tested model elaborated a planted fake clinical detail 50–83% of the time, and a mitigation prompt only halved this, leaving roughly 23% residual (Omar et al. 2025). Prompt hygiene alone is therefore insufficient. The constructive answer in the literature is grounding plus verifiable attribution: agentic and graph-RAG architectures have reached roughly 92–100% NCCN concordance *with* document-and-page citations (arXiv 2502.15698, preprint). That ceiling, however, was demonstrated by *other* architectures, not by OPL; OPL's own NCCN-concordance number in our benchmark is the overreach failure reported in §5, not this ceiling. The literature is nonetheless why OPL is built retrieval-first with PMID anchoring rather than relying on model self-consistency — and why a retraction-screening layer is required, since LLMs silently cite retracted oncology papers in roughly 10% of cases (Gu et al. 2025).

The v2.10 release introduces three mechanisms intended to couple the patient-facing brief to the evidence; they are described here as design, not as a validated fix, and whether they achieve that coupling in practice is exactly what the in-progress re-run (§5) tests. First, an **Evidence Contract**: integrators are specified to run *live, never from memory* — when a source is unreachable the system emits a fallback block rather than substituting the model's recollection, a mechanism intended to prevent silent offline degradation. Second, **gating the delivered brief**: earlier builds inspected side-files while the patient-facing document could drift free of them (a "computed-but-disconnected" gap), so the gates were changed to inspect the brief that is actually delivered. Third, **actionable-first ordering**: real options that already exist in the world — trials, next-line regimens, expanded-access pathways — are rendered *above* speculative candidates, so that Pain-A is literally presented before Pain-B, operationalizing the sequence the article argues is the discipline.

This contrasts instructively with the AI co-scientist systems that motivate the Pain-B ambition. Google's AI co-scientist (Gottweis et al. 2026) ranks novel hypotheses through an idea tournament — itself a useful discipline against raw generation — and FutureHouse's Robin (2026) ran a full hypothesis-to-experiment loop to surface a repurposing candidate (ripasudil for dry AMD) that humans had not. But both validate only in vitro and in organoid systems, and the co-scientist's authors are explicit that the system does not replace a research team or conduct science autonomously (Gottweis et al. 2026). OPL inverts their emphasis: where co-scientist systems optimize Pain-B novelty, OPL front-loads Pain-A retrieval-and-vetting — the tractable, patient-relevant job demonstrated near expert level by trial-matching work (Jin et al. 2024) — and fences Pain-B as clearly-labelled speculation downstream.

The method's own residual limits must be stated. Mitigation prompts reduce but never eliminate hallucination (roughly 23% residual; Omar et al. 2025), and the opposite failure is real: over-tight grounding can omit valid guideline-concordant options (PMC12017742), while any matcher is capped by registry quality, acutely so for ChiCTR (PMC10602811). No published architecture yet resolves the loose-versus-tight tension in oncology simultaneously; OPL's design choice — retrieve broadly, vet strictly, never silently drop, keep the patient as decision authority — is a defensible position within that tension, not an escape from it.

## 5. Empirical Evaluation: Benchmark Design and Current Results

We evaluated whether disciplined retrieval-first synthesis measurably improves a patient-facing brief using **synthetic cases and a prompt-driven adapter — not the production engine**. Several further scope constraints must be stated before any number is read. The adapter does *not* run OPL's full stack: there is no Wave-2 hypothesis tournament, no Wave-3/4 evidence and validation passes, and none of the deterministic Python gates; both multi-agent (`-full`) arms were reduced to synchronous calls, and the verifier is an LLM stand-in for the production registry. All five arms — baseline (single call), `mtb-anchor` (labelled `mtb-lite` in the source tables), `mtb-full`, `opl-anchor`, `opl-full` — share one reasoning model (MiniMax-M2.7) with identical temperature and retry budget, so the only contrast is the prompt corpus and multi-agent shape. Cases are synthetic, n=30 attempted per arm per surface (21–25 scorable per arm), across two surfaces: colorectal-cancer (CRC) therapy selection and NCCN decision concordance. Confidence intervals are correspondingly wide; we treat metric deltas below roughly 0.1 as noise. Crucially, the per-arm numbers immediately below are the *prior* (v1.5) prompt corpus — the "before"; the v2.10 re-coupling described in §4 is what a now-completed re-run tests, and its before→after deltas are reported at the end of this section.

On the CRC therapy-selection surface, the retrieval-first design produced a modest signal that rests primarily on the ranking metric. `opl-anchor` led nDCG@3 (0.679 vs 0.556 for baseline, a delta of 0.123 that clears the noise band) and therapy F1@3 (0.463 on the full denominator vs 0.393 for baseline; 0.555 vs 0.472 on scorable cases), with the lowest contraindicated-recommendation and molecular-context-error rates (both 0.040). We flag plainly that the F1@3 delta of 0.070 falls *within* our own stated noise band, so the positive CRC claim should be read as resting on nDCG@3, not F1@3. We also flag that all arms score low in absolute terms (best therapy F1@3 ~0.46 on the full denominator): the result is a relative ordering on a hard synthetic surface, not evidence of clinical adequacy. The selective-reporting caveat cuts the other way too: `opl-anchor` has a *worse* off-gold rate than baseline (0.880 vs 0.760), because it makes more recommendations — directly relevant to the overreach theme below. With those caveats, the ordering is consistent with — but does not isolate — the Pain-A claim; the adapter contains no live retrieval (the verifier is an LLM stand-in), so the gain reflects the OPL prompt design, not demonstrated retrieval grounding.

The benchmark also surfaced an honest counter-finding. On the NCCN concordance surface, **baseline won decisively** (0.400 strict scorable concordance vs 0.160 for `opl-anchor` and 0.125 for `opl-full`), because that surface rewards stopping to request missing discriminators, and the multi-expert pipelines instead fanned out into specific recommendations and were scored as unsafe overreach (0.438 for `opl-anchor`, 0.375 for `opl-full`, vs 0.125 baseline). The `-full` arms also underperformed their anchor twins on F1 and cost 7–10× the wall-time (187–268s vs ~25s baseline) with no clinical gain on this substrate. We report this plainly: on the present substrate the prior multi-expert shape overreaches on knowing when to stop — precisely the failure the v2.10 re-coupling is *intended* to address by placing actionable real options above speculative candidates and forcing gates to inspect the delivered brief. Whether it does so is unproven.

Two interpretive limits follow. First, a benchmark of this kind can show that disciplined retrieval-first prompting helps selection and can localize the overreach failure mode, but it cannot itself demonstrate that an architectural fix resolves that failure; that requires a re-run on the re-coupled prompts. Second, the field offers an external cautionary anchor that we treat fully in the discussion: the first randomized precision-oncology trial, and a negative one, was SHIVA01, whose failure was attributed to weak matching rules (Le Tourneau et al. 2015).

A v2.10 re-run on the re-coupled prompt corpus (n=30 × 5 arms × 2 surfaces, MiniMax-M2.7 at an 80K-token reasoning floor) is now complete, and the before→after picture is **mixed** — the kind of result that should temper rather than inflate the claim. On CRC therapy selection the disciplined retrieval-first design *strengthened*: `opl-anchor`'s therapy F1@3 (full denominator) rose from 0.463 to **0.507**, and its margin over baseline widened from +0.070 (inside the ≈0.1 noise band in v1.5) to **+0.113 — now clearing it**, so the Pain-A ranking edge on this surface is, post-fix, a signal rather than noise (`mtb-full` also improved markedly, 0.281→0.391). But the same re-run *regressed* `opl-anchor`'s nDCG@3 (0.679→0.569, now behind `mtb-anchor`'s 0.580), and — most consequentially — it did **not** resolve the safety-critical overreach: on NCCN decision concordance baseline still wins decisively (strict scorable 0.400→0.565 vs `opl-anchor` 0.235, `opl-full` 0.105), and the multi-expert arms' unsafe-overreach rate stayed high (`opl-full` 0.733). The "know-when-to-stop" failure the prior run surfaced persists. Two caveats bound this. First, the adapter is **prompt-driven**: it exercises the v2.10 *prompt corpus*, not the v2.10 engine-layer fixes (live-integrator wiring; gates that inspect the *delivered brief* rather than a parallel claims file; rendering real actionable options above speculative ones) that most directly target overreach — whether those resolve it requires a production `opl-cancer` engine run we have not yet executed. Second, the n and the synthetic substrate keep confidence intervals wide. Taken together: disciplined Pain-A retrieval-first prompting improved real-option ranking on CRC to a now-above-noise margin, while the overreach and the democratization payoff (§6) remain empirically open.

## 6. Discussion

The argument advanced here rests on an asymmetry that the article must state plainly: the literature licenses the *method* far more readily than the *payoff*. The case for discipline is strong. Large language models fabricate references most exactly in the rare, under-studied regime where founder-mode patients live — roughly 6% citation fabrication for well-covered conditions versus 28–29% for rarer ones (JMIR 2025) — and raw model concordance with guideline care degrades on precisely the recurrent disease that defines Sijbrandij's situation (Tsai et al. 2024). Against this, retrieval-grounded, attribution-bearing architectures push concordance toward ceiling, with reports of roughly 92–100% guideline adherence under verifiable document-and-page citation (arXiv 2502.15698, preprint) — though, as §4 notes, this was demonstrated by systems other than OPL. The decomposition the article defends — exhaust the real options first, fence the speculative leads, anchor every claim to a traceable source — is therefore well supported as engineering and as epistemics.

The *democratization* claim is the thinnest part of the evidence, and we own it as the central open question rather than a settled result. No published study demonstrates that an AI system closes the resource gap for an ordinary patient; every documented founder-mode success still required elite inputs — Fajgenbaum's MD training and laboratory access (Fajgenbaum et al. 2019), Giusti's infrastructure built on over half a billion dollars raised (HBS case 814026), Sijbrandij's CEO-level resourcing. That AI compresses the *labour* of a molecular tumor board (itself a dozen-plus specialist roles; CAN.HEAL Consortium 2025) is plausible; that this compression actually delivers real options to a non-elite patient is, at present, an untested hypothesis.

Two further limitations bound any benefit claim. First, the precision-oncology benefit on which the whole enterprise leans is associative, not causal: supportive cohorts are single-arm and selection-confounded, and the first randomized trial, SHIVA01, was negative (PFS 2.3 vs 2.0 months; Le Tourneau et al. 2015). Its failure was attributed — by the trialists' own post-hoc interpretation — to weak matching rules, which is itself the Pain-A discipline argument; but the discipline argument cannot be both the explanation for a negative trial and proof of benefit, and we do not treat it as the latter. Second, the trust boundary ultimately rests on the host model honestly transcribing the evidence it retrieves. Mitigation prompts roughly halve adversarial hallucination but leave a ~23% residual (Omar et al. 2025); models silently cite retracted oncology work in roughly 10% of cases without disclosing the retraction (Gu et al. 2025); and over-tight grounding introduces the inverse failure of omitting valid options (PMC12017742). No published architecture solves both failure modes simultaneously in oncology. Our own empirical evidence is correspondingly modest: a small-n (n=30 attempted/arm), synthetic-patient, prompt-driven adapter — not the production engine — that supports a narrow Pain-A ranking signal while independently surfacing the overreach the v2.10 re-coupling targets — and the completed re-run shows that overreach *persisted* at the prompt level (`opl-full` NCCN unsafe-overreach 0.733), with the engine-layer fix still unvalidated. A final bias must be named: the founder-mode existence proofs are selected survivors, and we cannot estimate how many comparably-resourced patients pursued founder mode and did not benefit.

These limits define the ethics. The system democratizes hypothesis generation and option-vetting, not bedside decisions: this is research support, not treatment advice, and the patient — with their clinician — is the sole decision authority. Even the authors of the AI co-scientist state plainly that it does not replace a research team or conduct science alone (Gottweis et al. 2026). Access and radical transparency follow as design principles rather than slogans. Pain-A maps to lawful but invisible options — FDA Expanded Access proceeds in roughly 99% of requests once the upstream gates of manufacturer consent and cost are cleared (Mayo Clin Proc Innov Qual Outcomes 2020), regional pathways such as Hainan Boao Lecheng have reportedly served more than 28,000 patients (Frontiers in Medicine 2025), and off-label use is legitimate precisely when compendium-graded (Conti et al. 2013) — so the value is surfacing and drafting, never forcing coverage or consent. Echoing Sijbrandij's own openness about his N-of-1 effort, every claim must be traceable to a verifiable record: one cannot trust a match one cannot trace. The honest residual — that today's evidence licenses the method but not yet the payoff — is itself the discipline this paper argues for.

## 7. Conclusion: From Privilege to Capability, Conditional on Discipline

Founder-mode against exhausted-options cancer is, at present, a proven *archetype* but an unproven *equity proposition*. The documented cases — Sijbrandij's recurrent osteosarcoma, Fajgenbaum's self-profiled idiopathic multicentric Castleman disease and sirolimus repurposing (Fajgenbaum et al. 2019), Giusti's multiple-myeloma infrastructure — suggest that a patient who becomes the principal investigator of their own case can sometimes find real options where the routing systems failed them. These are, however, selected survivors; we cannot estimate how many comparably-resourced patients pursued founder mode and did not benefit, and each documented win still required elite inputs (cf. §6). The contribution we argue AI can make is therefore narrower and more honest than the marketing temptation: not to manufacture the desperation that drove these patients, but to make the *discipline* that made their search legitimate — rather than dangerous — scalable.

That discipline is the load-bearing claim that survives scrutiny. Do Pain-A first and exhaustively: retrieve, match, and vet the real options that already exist — next-line therapy, NGS-matched drugs, trials, expanded access, evidence-graded off-label use. This is technically tractable today at near-expert level when grounded and attributable (Jin et al. 2024), and grounding with verifiable citation is what has pushed guideline concordance toward ceiling in other systems rather than leaving the patient with a fluent but fabricated answer. Only then, on the residual "actionable-but-no-drug" cohort that even fully resourced tumor boards leave stranded (Frost et al. 2022), should one fence Pain-B — grounded novel-lead generation, an idea-tournament rather than raw model output (Gottweis et al. 2026), held to the same evidentiary rigor that distinguishes a published N-of-1 from an anecdote (Batley et al. 2023). Anchor every claim to a live, traceable source; keep the patient and clinician as the sole decision authority. This sequence is what separates a democratized research team from quackery and false hope — the alternative, Pain-B without Pain-A vetting, is the failure mode that degrades founder-mode into stem-cell tourism (PMC8890798).

Our own evidence licenses the method, not the payoff, and saying so is itself the discipline this paper argues for. The benchmark suggests disciplined retrieval-first prompting improves real-option ranking, but independently surfaces the exact overreach — pushing past "need more information" — that the v2.10 re-coupling is intended to address; the completed before→after re-run sharpened the CRC ranking signal (opl-anchor therapy F1@3 0.463→0.507, now above noise) but did *not* resolve that overreach at the prompt level, and the field carries at least one negative randomized trial (Le Tourneau et al. 2015) whose failure was attributed to weak matching, the very gap Pain-A addresses.

Three things remain to be shown, and they define the future work. First, the central open question: a prospective demonstration that an *ordinary*, non-elite-resourced patient gains real options through such a system — the democratization claim is, as yet, untested, since every documented success still needed elite resources. Second, a single validated pipeline that integrates Pain-A and Pain-B, which no published architecture yet does. Third, the residual safety frontier: mitigation reduces but never eliminates fabrication (Omar et al. 2025), models still cite retracted oncology work silently (Gu et al. 2025), and over-tight grounding introduces its own failure of omitting valid options (PMC12017742) — no architecture demonstrably resolves both at once in oncology. Until these are closed, the honest position is that the evidence today licenses the *method*, not yet the *payoff*. Moving founder-mode from a privilege of the resourced few toward an accessible capability is a goal worth pursuing precisely because, and only as long as, it is pursued with that candor.
